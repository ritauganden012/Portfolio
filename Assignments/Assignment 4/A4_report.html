Final Reflection

As a woman, I felt a natural pull toward the abortion dataset. I've always grappled with the moral complexities surrounding abortion—an issue layered with nuance. In exploring this data, I chose not to manipulate or augment it, as I wanted to observe women's behaviors as they relate to abortion care.

In modern society, young women are often encouraged to embrace their sexuality, to own their bodies and pleasures—ideally without repercussions. But this dataset tells a different story. It reveals that increasingly younger women, particularly those aged 15 to 19, are seeking abortion care, sometimes to the extent of traveling out of state to access it. Imagine that: crossing state lines, entering an unfamiliar clinic, meeting a doctor they have never seen before, and hoping they will be cared for—all because abortion care is unavailable where they live. That, to me, is deeply provocative.

Even more unsettling is the reality that these young women are terminating pregnancies—ending potential lives. When abortion rates decline in a given city, the data often shows that there was already an established demand for these services. Restrictions don’t eliminate the need; they shift where and how care is accessed.

One of my primary storytelling choices was to frame my findings with thought-provoking titles, positioning them as claims. For viewers who already hold strong beliefs, these claims may serve to reinforce their stance. But for others, they may challenge perspectives and invite deeper engagement with the data. My goal was not just to present numbers but to provoke thought, spark dialogue, and encourage reflection on the lived realities behind these statistics.
